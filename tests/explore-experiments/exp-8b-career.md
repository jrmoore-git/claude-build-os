# Explore: How should a senior IC transition to engineering management?

3. Do not become manager yet; ask for an external hire while you act as interim team lead and preserve your IC trajectory.
4. Make it a 90-day trial management rotation with a written reversibility plan so both you and the company can test fit without career damage.
5. Reframe the problem entirely: the team may not need a classic manager at all, but an operating cadence plus rotating technical stewardship and skip-level support.
6. Become manager but deliberately stop being the technical center of gravity, using the two more senior domain experts as formal area leads with decision rights.
7. Skip internal mentorship and instead build a peer manager circle across the company to compare notes weekly and normalize first-time manager mistakes.

10. Do the uncomfortable thing: refuse the role unless your skip-level commits to monthly coaching, a shadow period, and authority over promotions and performance decisions.

## Direction: Run a 90-day player-coach trial with formal tech leads and an explicit exit ramp

## The Argument
The most interesting move is not “become a manager” or “don’t.” It’s to turn this into a **bounded experiment**: you step in as manager for 90 days, but with a written operating model that prevents the two classic failure modes—becoming a bad full-time manager because you cling to coding, or becoming a weak manager because you secretly want to stay an IC.

This direction differs on multiple axes: it’s a **player-coach transition**, not a clean promotion; your technical involvement is **strategic and narrowly scoped**, not broad hands-on ownership; the first 90 days focus on **team relationships plus decision-rights redesign**, not process cleanup; the mentorship model is a **peer manager group plus skip-level coaching**, not self-directed guessing; and risk management is a **trial period with reversibility**, not a permanent identity shift.

### Why now?
Because the team has already spent **3 months without a manager** and is currently reporting to the skip-level. That changes the situation materially from even two years ago. The team is already operating in an abnormal state, so introducing a structured interim management model is politically and operationally possible now in a way it usually isn’t. Also, modern engineering work is increasingly specialized; your two reports are more senior in specific domains, which means the old model—manager as top technical decider—is less viable today. Management now works better as **coordination, clarity, hiring, performance, and interface management**, while technical authority can sit with domain leads.

### What’s the workaround?
Without this structure, companies do one of three things:
- A senior IC becomes the “unofficial manager” while still carrying major project load.
- The skip-level continues to absorb management overhead and the team runs half-autonomously.
- An external manager is hired cold, creating trust debt and slower technical context acquisition.

Those workarounds prove demand: the team needs management now. They also reveal the real constraint: nobody wants to lose technical momentum or miscast a strong IC permanently.

### Moment of abandonment
People quit this transition around **week 6 to 10**. Early on, the promotion feels flattering and manageable. Then the calendar fills with 1:1s, hiring, conflict handling, planning, and cross-functional meetings. The new manager still tries to review every design and code path, burns out, and disappoints both sides. Design backward from that failure point: make broad technical ownership impossible from day one.

So you explicitly assign the two senior domain experts as **area leads** with visible decision rights. Your role becomes: staffing, prioritization, execution health, stakeholder alignment, and coaching. You still stay technical by owning one narrow slice—architecture reviews for one platform area, or one weekly design forum—but you stop being the team’s default escalation for all engineering decisions.

### 10x on which axis?
This is 10x better on **reversibility**. Most first-time management moves are sticky and identity-laden; people endure too long because stepping back feels like failure. A trial with an exit ramp produces better decisions because it lowers emotional distortion. You and the company both get signal without pretending certainty.

### Adjacent analogy
This is how medicine often handles responsibility expansion: a **chief resident** or acting lead role bridges individual expertise and supervisory accountability before a permanent identity shift. The mechanism is supervised authority plus bounded duration. It transfers because both environments are high-skill, status-sensitive, and dangerous when the best practitioner is assumed to be the best manager.

## First Move
Next week, ask your skip-level for a **written 90-day trial charter** with four points:

1. You become acting engineering manager for 90 days.
2. Two senior teammates are designated formal domain leads with clear decision ownership.
3. You keep one small technical lane only—no more than 20% of time.
4. At day 75, you and the skip-level decide among three paths: continue as manager, extend trial, or return to IC without penalty.

Then meet the team and say exactly what changes: you now own 1:1s, performance, prioritization, and cross-functional alignment; domain leads own technical decisions in their areas; you are not the new bottleneck.

## Why This Beats the Alternatives
**Obvious approach 1: Just take the manager role permanently.**  
Too much ambiguity, no safety valve, and you will likely overcompensate by staying too technical.

**Obvious approach 2: Stay an IC and wait for a manager hire.**  
The team already has a gap. Deferring creates drift, and an external hire will enter with less trust and less context than you.

**Obvious approach 3: Be a full player-coach indefinitely.**  
That is the most seductive and most damaging option. It sounds balanced but usually becomes two jobs done at 70%.

## Biggest Risk
The company says “trial,” but behaves as if the decision is already permanent. If reversibility is fake, you’ll optimize for optics instead of learning, cling to technical work to preserve status, and the experiment will fail for political reasons rather than managerial fit.

## The Argument
The question is wrong because it assumes the team’s need for a manager should be solved by converting the strongest nearby IC. Those are different problems.

The team’s immediate problem is **management coverage**: 1:1s, performance, hiring, prioritization, conflict handling, stakeholder buffering, and restoring operating cadence after 3 months without a manager. None of that requires sacrificing your best technical anchor. In fact, on a team already destabilized by manager loss and no onboarding system, a first-time manager is the riskier choice.

**Why now?** Because the previous manager has already been gone 3 months. The team is no longer in a short-term gap; it is operating without stable people management. That makes “learn management by doing” less attractive, not more. The company has also shown there is no support structure for a new manager, so the transition cost would land entirely on the team.

**What’s the workaround?** Companies usually do one of three things when they don’t do this: the skip-level absorbs the team, a senior IC informally handles glue work, or the team muddles through. All three are already visible here, and all three are lossy. The better workaround is explicit: hire or assign an experienced EM, and keep the senior IC in a technical leadership lane.

**10x on which axis?** Risk reduction. This direction is 10x better on avoiding compounded failure: losing technical leverage *and* getting weak management at the same time.

**Adjacent analogy:** In medicine, the best surgeon is not automatically made head nurse to solve a staffing issue. You fill the management gap with management capability and preserve scarce specialist skill. Engineering makes this mistake constantly because management is misread as the default “next step.”

Mechanically, this means:
- Put an experienced EM over the team.
- Make the senior IC the explicit technical lead/staff anchor for architecture, technical planning, and mentoring.
- Give the two more domain-senior engineers formal ownership in their areas so expertise hierarchy is acknowledged, not threatened.
- Build a clean interface: manager owns people/performance/process; technical lead owns engineering direction and technical quality.

This is not “avoid growth.” It is choosing the right growth path. If the senior IC later wants management, they can pursue it when the company can actually support it, or after observing a good EM in action.

## First Move
Tell the skip-level this in one sentence: **“Do not solve this vacancy by converting me into a first-time manager right now; hire or assign an experienced EM, and I will take formal technical leadership for the team in the meantime.”**

Then write a one-page role split with three columns:
1. **Manager owns** — 1:1s, performance, compensation input, conflict resolution, staffing, stakeholder escalation.
2. **I own** — technical roadmap, architecture decisions, execution health, mentoring, code quality.
3. **Domain seniors own** — area-specific technical decisions and review standards.

This changes the conversation from “should I become manager?” to “what operating model does this team need?”

## Why This Beats the Alternatives
The obvious approach is promoting the senior IC because they are credible and available. That’s exactly why it’s dangerous. Availability is not readiness; technical respect is not management skill; and a team with no manager for 3 months is not a safe classroom.

It also beats the earlier kinds of answers people usually give:
- **Not a player-coach trial:** that still asks one person to absorb two jobs and learn management under load.
- **Not a formal tech-lead transition plan:** that still centers the IC becoming manager.
- **Not mentorship-first:** coaching helps, but it does not replace actual management experience or structural support.

This direction differs at the root: **the promotion itself is the wrong intervention.** Solve the team’s management deficit directly.

## Biggest Risk
The company may refuse to hire or reassign an experienced EM quickly, either due to headcount or politics. If that happens, pressure will snap back to “just have the senior IC do it.” If leadership lacks the will to separate technical leadership from people management, this direction dies.

## The Argument
The question is wrong because it assumes the bottleneck is lack of a manager. It probably isn’t. What changed is that the previous manager left three months ago, and the team has been running on borrowed institutional structure ever since. That creates pain: unclear prioritization, weak 1:1 cadence, unclear escalation paths, no performance process owner, and too much load on the skip-level. Those are management *functions*. They do not automatically require turning your best senior IC into a people manager.

So the right move is to **separate “does this team need management work?” from “should this person become a manager?”**. Solve the first before committing to the second.

The pattern: keep the senior IC in-role technically, and stand up a minimal team operating system owned jointly by the skip-level and the team. Specifically:
- skip-level remains formal people manager,
- senior IC owns delivery cadence and technical integration,
- the two domain-senior engineers own decisions in their areas,
- a weekly management checklist replaces absent management infrastructure.

Why now? Because after three months without a manager, you have enough signal to identify the real failures. If velocity is down because decisions stall or nobody owns staffing/performance/admin, promoting the IC won’t magically fix that; it just changes who absorbs the chaos. You now know enough to design around the actual gaps.

What’s the workaround people are doing without this? Usually: ad hoc Slack escalations, skip-level heroics, senior engineers informally mentoring juniors, and planning happening in incident mode. That works briefly, then burns everyone out and makes a management transition look falsely attractive.

10x axis: **clarity per unit of role disruption**. You want the most stability with the least irreversible career change.

Three ways the question is wrong because:
1. **The question is wrong because management may be a systems gap, not a person gap.**
2. **The question is wrong because a first-time manager without onboarding is being asked to compensate for company process debt.**
3. **The question is wrong because “someone has to manage” does not imply “the strongest IC should stop being an IC.”**

## First Move
Within the next 5 business days, the skip-level convenes a 45-minute team design meeting and explicitly assigns four accountabilities:
1. **People management** stays with skip-level.
2. **Delivery cadence** goes to the senior IC.
3. **Domain decision ownership** goes to the two more senior specialists in their areas.
4. **Operating rhythm** is fixed: weekly staff meeting, biweekly planning review, monthly performance/feedback pass, written escalation path.

Then document this on one page and run it immediately. The point is not “process.” The point is to expose whether the missing thing is actually management, or just unmanaged work.

## Why This Beats the Alternatives
The obvious approach—promote the senior IC into engineering management—confuses local need with individual fit. It risks losing a strong technical contributor, under-supporting a first-time manager, and creating status problems with the two domain-senior reports.

Previous direction 1 (player-coach trial) still accepts the bad premise: that the answer is to test-drive the IC in management. It changes the commitment level, but not the assumption.

Previous direction 2 (hire a manager; don’t turn the senior IC into one) solves for role purity, but it still assumes a manager-shaped hole exists. Maybe it doesn’t. You may need operating structure now, not a requisition cycle and an external hire ramp.

This direction beats both because it targets the actual failure mode first. If the team stabilizes under explicit cadence and split accountabilities, you just saved a mis-promotion or unnecessary hire. If it doesn’t, you get sharper evidence about what manager capacity is truly missing.

## Biggest Risk
The biggest risk is that the skip-level does not actually have the bandwidth or discipline to remain the formal people manager while the operating system gets installed. If they half-own it, you get the worst of both worlds: no manager and more process.

## Bet A: 90-Day Player-Coach Trial
A bounded trial is the direct answer if you’re genuinely open to management but want real signal before making it sticky. You become acting manager for 90 days, keep one narrow technical lane, and explicitly shift broad technical authority to formal domain leads. This works because it tests managerial fit under real conditions while preventing the classic failure mode of doing two jobs badly. It also lowers politics and identity risk by making reversibility explicit.
**First move:** Ask your skip-level for a written 90-day charter: acting EM role, two formal area leads, max 20% technical time, and a day-75 decision to continue, extend, or return to IC without penalty.
**Sacrifice:** You give up short-term technical output and some status as the team’s default technical center of gravity.

## Bet B: Hire an Experienced EM, Keep IC Leadership
If the team’s real problem is management coverage, solve that directly instead of converting the strongest nearby IC. Bring in or assign an experienced engineering manager, while you remain the staff-plus technical anchor and the two domain experts get formal decision rights. This works because it preserves scarce technical leverage, restores coaching/performance quality faster, and avoids teaching first-time management on a team already running without support. It is the cleanest role split: manager owns people and process; you own technical direction.
**First move:** Tell your skip-level: “Don’t solve this vacancy by converting me right now; assign or hire an experienced EM, and I’ll take formal technical leadership meanwhile.” Then draft a one-page ownership split.
**Sacrifice:** You give up the near-term opportunity to test management yourself and some influence over how the team-management interface gets shaped.

## Bet C: Install an Operating System, No Manager Yet
If the pain is really missing cadence, not missing a title, don’t force a career change or hire immediately. Keep the skip-level as formal people manager, keep yourself hands-on as delivery/technical integrator, and assign the domain specialists explicit ownership. Spend the first 90 days on operating mechanisms: planning rhythm, escalation paths, feedback loops, and decision clarity. This works if a team of six can function with lightweight structure and shared accountability instead of a conventional manager. It’s the least disruptive way to test whether a manager-shaped hole actually exists.
**First move:** Convene a 45-minute design session next week to assign people management, delivery cadence, domain ownership, and a fixed meeting/review rhythm; document it on one page.
**Sacrifice:** You give up speed toward a conventional org structure and may delay learning whether dedicated management capacity is ultimately unavoidable.
