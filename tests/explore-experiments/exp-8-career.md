# Explore: How should a senior IC transition to engineering management?

## Direction 1

## Brainstorm
- Take the manager role directly as a conventional player-coach: keep 30–40% hands-on coding while learning core management through 1:1s, planning, and feedback loops.
- Accept the role but immediately build an internal support system: a skip-level mentor, a peer manager circle, and a lightweight operating cadence for the team.
- Decline the manager role and push for an external manager hire while you remain technical lead, protecting your IC edge and avoiding untrained management.
- Propose a 90-day trial period as acting manager with explicit success criteria and a clean path back to IC if the fit is wrong.
- Reframe the problem entirely: the team may not need a classic manager yet, but a rotating leadership structure plus strong PM/tech lead coordination.
- Become a strategic tech leader rather than a coding manager: stop owning implementation, own architecture, hiring, staffing, and decision quality.
- Do the uncomfortable thing: fully delegate technical authority in the two domains where your reports are stronger, and measure your success by their leverage, not your expertise.
- Create your own manager onboarding program from scratch: a written team charter, decision rules, meeting cadence, escalation paths, and feedback norms in the first month.
- Use an external coach instead of internal advice, treating the transition like executive ramp-up rather than “learning on the job.”
- Split the role formally: you serve as people manager for six months while appointing a senior engineer as designated technical lead for day-to-day engineering decisions.

## Direction: Formal player-coach trial with delegated technical authority

## The Argument
The best move is not “become a manager” in the abstract. It is to become the team’s **acting manager for 90 days under an explicit trial**, while **deliberately giving technical authority away** in the domains where your reports are stronger and keeping only **strategic technical involvement**. That combination is the interesting move because it solves the real failure mode: senior ICs don’t fail at management because they lack intelligence; they fail because they keep trying to prove technical value while doing a new job with no scaffolding.

### 1. Why now?
Three things make this necessary now in a way it wasn’t two years ago.

First, the team has already been manager-less for three months and is reporting to a skip-level. That creates ambiguity debt: feedback, prioritization, career conversations, and conflict resolution are probably happening inconsistently or not at all. You can coast like this for a short period; after a quarter, the cost compounds.

Second, your team is small—six people—which makes a **trial management model** feasible. At this size, you can establish management basics quickly without a full organizational machine.

Third, modern engineering teams now depend more on coordination quality than on one hero technologist knowing everything. The presence of two domain-senior reports means the old model—manager as top technical authority—isn’t just unnecessary, it’s counterproductive. Today, it is possible to manage effectively by orchestrating expertise rather than owning it.

### 2. What’s the workaround?
Without this structure, people do three workarounds:
- The skip-level informally manages the team.
- The strongest IC becomes the de facto coordinator without authority.
- Fires get handled ad hoc while career development and process rot are ignored.

Those workarounds prove demand: the team clearly needs management. They also reveal the constraint: nobody wants to commit to a permanent role change without confidence or support.

### 3. Moment of abandonment
People abandon the IC-to-manager transition around weeks 6–12, when the calendar fills up, technical context starts slipping, and they feel mediocre at both jobs. That is the quitting point.

Design backward from that. The answer is not “try harder.” The answer is to **remove the identity trap**:
- Make the role explicitly a 90-day trial.
- State in advance what success looks like.
- Reduce coding expectations before management load expands.
- Publicly assign technical ownership to others in their domains.

This prevents the classic collapse where you silently do two full-time jobs until both suffer.

### 4. 10x on which axis?
This approach is **10x better on reversibility**.

Not confidence. Not speed. Reversibility.

That matters most because the biggest hidden cost here is not trying management—it’s getting stuck in a bad version of it. A formal trial with a return path lets you commit seriously without pretending this is irreversible. On every other axis, it is good enough:
- Technical continuity: preserved via delegated domain leads.
- Team stability: improved because someone owns people/process.
- Learning: accelerated by real responsibility.
- Career risk: capped by explicit review.

If reversibility is missing, fear drives half-commitment and the transition fails.

### 5. Adjacent analogy
This is how medical systems use **acting chief resident** or supervised transition roles: real authority, bounded duration, explicit evaluation, and distributed expertise. The mechanism is not “learn first, do later.” It is “take the seat under guardrails, with seniors retaining specialty authority.” That transfers directly here. Management is also an apprenticeship role best learned in live conditions, but only if scope and fallback are clear.

## First Move
Next week, meet with your skip-level and propose this exact structure:

- You become **acting engineering manager for 90 days**.
- At day 0, appoint the two more senior domain experts as **named technical owners** for their areas.
- Your role shifts to people management, planning, prioritization, hiring, and cross-team communication, with **one bounded technical contribution** only: architecture/review on the highest-leverage project.
- Define 4 success criteria: team clarity, delivery predictability, 1:1 cadence and growth plans, and stakeholder satisfaction.
- Set a day-45 checkpoint and a day-90 go/no-go decision, including a clean return to IC if needed.

Then tell the team plainly: “I am not becoming the top expert in every domain; I am becoming accountable for team effectiveness.”

## Why This Beats the Alternatives
The obvious “just become manager and keep coding a lot” fails because it preserves your old identity and guarantees overload.

The obvious “wait for an external hire” fails because the team already has a management vacuum, and external managers ramp slowly—especially without onboarding infrastructure.

The obvious “fully stop technical work immediately” is too abrupt and needlessly threatens your credibility and confidence. Strategic technical involvement is enough; total detachment is not required on day one.

## Biggest Risk
The company may refuse to honor the trial as truly reversible. If “acting manager” becomes a political label for a permanent role with no way back, then the whole design breaks. Reversibility is not a nice-to-have here; it is the mechanism that makes honest commitment possible.

## Direction: You become manager, but ban yourself from being primary technical approver for 6 months

## ERRC Grid
| Eliminate | Reduce | Raise | Create |
|-----------|--------|-------|--------|
| The assumption that the new manager must also remain the technical hub and final code/design authority | Your individual coding load and heroics—do just enough technical work to stay fluent | Delegation, manager operating cadence, and visible empowerment of the two domain seniors | A **management transition firewall**: a time-boxed rule that separates people authority from technical gatekeeping so the team can rewire around a real manager, not a super-IC |

## The Argument
The right move is not “become a manager and try to keep doing your old job at 70%.” That fails. The team already spent 3 months without a manager; what they lack is not more technical throughput, but coordination, feedback, prioritization, and decision clarity.

So take the management role now—but with one hard structural rule: for six months, you are **not** the primary approver for architecture or code in the main domains. The two more senior domain experts become explicit technical approvers in their areas. You still attend reviews, ask questions, and shape technical strategy, but you do not sit in the critical path.

**Why now?**  
Because the vacancy has already lasted 3 months. Informal management has likely emerged anyway—unevenly, invisibly, and without accountability. If you step in without breaking your old technical centrality, the team will continue routing everything through you. That recreates the vacuum with a title change.

**What’s the workaround?**  
Most senior ICs who “transition” end up as shadow tech leads plus part-time therapist plus escalation sink. They keep the hard technical decisions, do 1:1s on top, and slowly become bad at both. The team becomes dependent, and the new manager never learns real management.

**10x on which axis?**  
**Managerial bandwidth.** This approach multiplies how much actual management you can do by removing approval bottlenecks and protecting time for people, prioritization, and cross-functional alignment.

**Adjacent analogy:**  
In medicine, new attendings do not scale by personally performing every task; they create safe delegation protocols and supervise through systems. The point is not abdication. It is controlled transfer of operational authority so the unit functions without one heroic operator.

This works especially well in your context because two reports are already more senior in specific domains. Use that. Their expertise is not a threat to your authority; it is the foundation for it. Your legitimacy as a manager will come from clarity, fairness, staffing decisions, performance judgment, and team output—not from winning every technical argument.

## First Move
Book a 60-minute meeting with your skip-level and the team this week and announce a **role reset effective Monday**:

1. You take the engineering manager seat.
2. Domain A senior becomes design/code approver for Domain A; Domain B senior for Domain B.
3. All project status, staffing, prioritization, feedback, and stakeholder escalation route through you.
4. You will not be required approval on normal technical changes for 6 months.

Then schedule six weekly 1:1s, create a decision-rights document, and publish the new approval map in the team channel.

## Why This Beats the Alternatives
The obvious approach—become manager and stay heavily hands-on—preserves your comfort, not team performance. It delays delegation, confuses authority, and traps you in double duty.

Hiring an external manager may work eventually, but it ignores the immediate leadership gap and wastes your organizational context when the team already needs stable direction.

A player-coach setup with delegated authority was the old tempting answer. It still leaves you partly in the technical center. My direction is stricter: it **removes** you from primary technical approval entirely for a fixed window. That is a deeper structural break.

A formal trial period sounds safe, but it often causes everyone to wait and see. The team needs commitment and clear routing now, not provisional leadership theater.

## Biggest Risk
You fail to uphold the firewall and keep getting pulled back in as the de facto senior IC. If you stay the person everyone waits on for technical approval, the transition dies.

## Direction: Shadow manager apprenticeship under skip-level

## ERRC Grid
| Eliminate | Reduce | Raise | Create |
|-----------|--------|-------|--------|
| The assumption that you must accept a title before learning the work | Immediate identity shift from top IC to full-time manager | Deliberate practice, observation, and feedback on management behaviors | A no-regrets apprenticeship layer between IC and EM, with live reps but buffered authority |

## The Argument
The best transition is **not to become manager immediately**. It is to spend one quarter as a **shadow manager apprentice** under your skip-level, then decide based on evidence.

This works because your team has already gone **3 months without a dedicated manager**. That changed the situation. The urgent problem is not “put someone in the box on the org chart.” The urgent problem is restoring management coverage without making a rushed bet on an untrained first-time manager. A quarter of apprenticeship is now possible because the team has already tolerated interim structure; another 8–12 weeks used intentionally is far safer than forcing a permanent role transition tomorrow.

Without this, people usually use a bad workaround: they promote the strongest IC and hope instincts transfer, or they leave the skip-level overloaded and the team under-managed. Both create hidden damage: weak 1:1s, vague performance expectations, conflict avoidance, and technical overreach from a manager trying to prove they still “have it.”

This direction is **10x on learning velocity**. You get real management reps—1:1 agendas, feedback drafts, planning, stakeholder updates, hiring debriefs—without carrying full irreversible accountability before you know whether you’re good at it or want it.

Adjacent analogy: this is how high-stakes professions handle role change. **Medical residency and cockpit right-seat training** don’t hand someone full command because they were great at the previous job. They create supervised reps in the real environment until judgment catches up to responsibility.

Here is the structure:
- For 12 weeks, your skip-level remains formal manager.
- You run weekly 1:1s in shadow mode, draft feedback, facilitate planning, and lead staff communication.
- Skip-level reviews your notes, joins selected conversations, and retains comp/performance authority.
- You stay partly hands-on technically, but only on bounded work that does not make you the team bottleneck.
- At the end, there is a binary decision: take the EM role, extend apprenticeship with a narrow gap list, or stay senior IC and trigger a manager search.

This solves your real fear too: losing technical edge. During apprenticeship you do not sever your IC identity. You test whether you can derive energy from manager work before making the leap.

## First Move
Book a 45-minute meeting with your skip-level this week and propose a **written 12-week shadow-manager charter**. It should name:
1. what you will run immediately: all 1:1s, sprint planning, stakeholder status;
2. what remains with skip-level: performance ratings, compensation, formal HR actions;
3. how feedback works: 30-minute weekly debrief after your management reps;
4. the decision date: week 12, yes/no on formal EM transition.

Then tell the team exactly what changes Monday: “I will run our weekly 1:1s and planning cadence for the next quarter; [skip-level] remains your formal manager while I apprentice into the role.”

## Why This Beats the Alternatives
The obvious approach—just promote the senior IC—confuses technical credibility with managerial readiness. It gives the team certainty on paper and uncertainty in practice.

Hiring a manager immediately may solve people management, but it throws away your institutional trust and forces the team to absorb an outsider while already unstable.

The previous directions fail for a different reason:
- **Formal player-coach with delegated authority** still makes you manager now. It asks you to learn management while already accountable for it.
- **Become manager but ban yourself from technical approval** is still an immediate promotion with artificial constraints. It treats the symptom—over-involvement—rather than the core issue: untested management capability.

This apprenticeship is structurally better because it changes the entry path, keeps technical involvement bounded rather than severed, focuses the first 90 days on relationship and skill-building through supervised reps, uses an internal mentor model, and preserves a real off-ramp.

## Biggest Risk
Your skip-level may not have the time or discipline to actively mentor you. If they treat “shadow mode” as informal osmosis instead of structured coaching, this collapses into ambiguous pseudo-management and frustrates everyone.
