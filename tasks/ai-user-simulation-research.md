# AI-Driven User Simulation and Feedback Loop Compression: How Companies Test Products Before Shipping

Companies and AI teams have fundamentally transformed the speed and efficiency of product validation by deploying large language models as synthetic users to simulate real customer interactions before deployment. This approach compresses months of traditional user research and testing into weeks or days, enabling teams to iterate rapidly through multiple design and feature variations, detect usability issues early, and validate core assumptions about user behavior within controlled environments. The underlying research demonstrates that LLM-generated personas can achieve alignment with real user responses in the range of 85–95% depending on the methodology employed, though significant challenges persist around consistency, demographic bias, and the risk of model collapse when synthetic data feeds back into training loops without careful management. This comprehensive report examines the frameworks, methodologies, reliability research, and real-world implementations of AI-driven user simulation across academia and industry, focusing on developments from 2024 through 2026.

## Foundational Research and Theoretical Underpinnings

The modern practice of using large language models as synthetic users traces its conceptual roots to a seminal 2022 paper that fundamentally challenged how researchers think about LLM biases. Rather than treating biases within models as uniform properties, researchers demonstrated that GPT-3's biases are fine-grained and demographically correlated, and crucially, that proper conditioning causes models to accurately emulate response distributions from diverse human subpopulations[18]. This work introduced the concept of "algorithmic fidelity," defined as the degree to which a language model's outputs accurately reflect the complex patterns of ideas, attitudes, and socio-cultural contexts found in human populations. The researchers created "silicon samples" by conditioning models on thousands of socio-demographic backstories derived from real human participants in large-scale surveys conducted in the United States, then demonstrated that the information contained in these models goes far beyond surface similarity, instead reflecting nuanced, multifaceted relationships between ideas, attitudes, and socio-cultural context[18]. This foundational insight transformed the perception of LLM simulation from a theoretical curiosity into a practical tool for research and product development.

Building on this foundation, researchers have developed a growing body of evidence that LLMs can simulate human economic behavior—termed "homo silicus"—across various scenarios, allowing teams to explore economic theories and behaviors through computational simulations. By endowing these models with specific preferences, information, and scenarios, researchers can observe their choices and gain insights into human decision-making processes[12]. Experiments with LLMs have successfully replicated and extended findings from traditional behavioral economics studies, offering a cost-effective and rapid method to pilot studies and explore variations in experimental design[12]. This has direct applications to product teams: market research surveys can be conducted at scale using LLM-based synthetic consumers, with responses reflecting well-known consumer behavior patterns such as price sensitivity and brand preference[12]. Critically, the responses to market research prompts provide realistic estimates of willingness to pay for products and features, with estimates aligning closely to those obtained from actual consumer surveys[12].

The transition from observing that LLMs can simulate human behavior to operationalizing this capability for product development required advances in how researchers think about persona construction and behavioral simulation. A major 2023 paper introduced "generative agents"—computational software agents that simulate believable human behavior through an architecture extending large language models with mechanisms to store a complete record of the agent's experiences using natural language, synthesize those memories over time into higher-level reflections, and retrieve them dynamically to plan behavior[27]. The paper demonstrated this through an interactive sandbox environment populated by 25 agents, where starting from only a single user-specified notion that one agent wanted to throw a Valentine's Day party, the agents autonomously spread invitations over two days, made new acquaintances, asked each other out on dates to the party, and coordinated to show up together at the right time[27]. This work established architectural and interaction patterns for enabling believable simulations of human behavior by fusing large language models with computational, interactive agents[27]. The significance of this work lies not merely in its technical accomplishment but in demonstrating that LLM-based agents could exhibit emergent social behaviors—patterns that arise from the interactions between agents but could not be predicted by examining individual agents in isolation.

## Frameworks and Methodologies for AI-Driven User Simulation

The practical implementation of AI-driven user simulation in product testing has generated several competing but complementary frameworks, each addressing different aspects of the simulation challenge. One of the most widely adopted approaches is the Dynamic Persona Refinement Framework (DPRF), which treats persona generation not as a one-shot task but as a data-driven optimization process[9]. DPRF operates through an iterative feedback loop by first prompting an LLM role-playing agent with an initial persona to generate behavioral outputs, then comparing these outputs against ground-truth data from target individuals through a behavior analysis agent that identifies divergences and automatically revises the persona profile[9]. This process repeats over successive iterations, progressively refining the persona to better capture the cognitive and behavioral traits of the target. Testing DPRF across five different LLMs on four diverse behavior-prediction scenarios—formal debates, social media posts with mental health issues, public interviews, and movie reviews—demonstrated that the framework consistently improves behavioral alignment considerably over baseline personas and generalizes across models and scenarios[9]. Crucially, personas refined through this framework enhance performance not only in semantic similarity but also in structural fidelity, indicating a deeper and more authentic behavioral alignment[9].

The persona refinement framework comprises a three-step process: behavior generation, divergence analysis, and persona refinement[9]. The behavior generation step prompts an LLM role-playing agent with an initial persona profile to generate behavioral outputs for a given scenario. The divergence analysis step employs a separate behavior analysis agent to compare these outputs against ground-truth data and identify misalignments. The persona refinement step then uses these identified divergences to automatically revise the persona profile, which feeds back into the next iteration[9]. This iterative approach has proven effective at optimizing alignment between agent behavior and human ground truth across diverse domains, laying groundwork for creating truly personalized AI assistants that can dynamically and continuously adapt to their users' unique behaviors, preferences, and cognitive styles[9].

A complementary approach gaining significant adoption in product teams involves using the OCEAN model—the Five-Factor personality model encompassing Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism—to induce specific personality traits in LLM-based agents[42]. Recent research demonstrates that prompt-based persona induction using the OCEAN model significantly influences LLM agent task planning and decision-making[42]. Experiments inducing each personality trait individually in either the forward direction (e.g., extraverted) or reverse direction (e.g., introverted) revealed distinct task-selection patterns aligned with induced OCEAN attributes, with effects being more pronounced in more capable models[42]. This systematic approach enables teams to create reproducible, measurable variations in simulated user behavior, which proves valuable for testing how products might be perceived by different personality types or user archetypes.

The Synthetic Users platform, developed as a commercial product but grounded in peer-reviewed research, employs what the company terms a "chain-of-feeling" approach—combining emotional states with OCEAN personality traits—to produce more human-like, realistic user responses and yield richer user experience insights[12][6]. Rather than using a simple chat interface, Synthetic Users deploys a multi-agent architecture where AI participants develop individual personality profiles based on the OCEAN model and maintain full context and continuity across every interview, capabilities that general-purpose AI tools cannot reliably achieve[6]. The platform advertises a "parity score" of 85–92% depending on audience type, meaning that responses from synthetic users align with real human responses at that level[6]. This metric provides teams with a quantifiable sense of when and where the synthetic simulation is likely to break down, enabling more informed decisions about which parts of product testing can confidently rely on synthetic users versus which require real user feedback.

For conversational AI testing specifically, the DeepEval framework introduced the ConversationSimulator tool, which enables simulation of full conversations between a fake user and a chatbot, unlike single-turn synthesis which generates regular test cases representing atomic LLM interactions[14]. The ConversationSimulator uses scenario and user description information to simulate back-and-forth exchanges with a chatbot, with the resulting dialogue used to create conversational test cases for evaluation[14]. This represents an important capability because conversational coherence and multi-turn consistency represent failure modes that only emerge across multiple turns—single-turn evaluation cannot adequately capture these dynamics.

## Compressing Build-Test-Learn Cycles Using Synthetic Users

The fundamental advantage of synthetic user simulation lies in its dramatic compression of the build-test-learn cycle that has historically constrained product development speed. In traditional approaches, product teams must recruit real users through time-consuming and expensive processes, conduct moderated or unmoderated testing sessions, analyze results, iterate, and repeat—a process that typically requires weeks or months to generate sufficient feedback for confident decision-making[5]. By contrast, teams employing LLM-powered synthetic users can generate test results in days or even hours, enable multiple rounds of iteration within a single development cycle, and stress-test assumptions at scale without the logistical overhead of participant recruitment and scheduling[5]. This compression creates a competitive advantage in fast-moving markets where the speed of learning directly correlates with the ability to capture market opportunities.

One commercial application of this principle is TestPilot, which uses AI-simulated marketplace environments to accelerate brand research. Rather than conducting traditional focus groups that might take weeks to recruit, schedule, and analyze, TestPilot brings actual consumers into simulated retail environments on platforms like Amazon and Walmart, uses AI to analyze every click and pause within those simulated experiences, and delivers comprehensive reports within days[26]. According to TestPilot's CEO, brand research can be so expensive and time-consuming that even the biggest marketers limit themselves to a handful of research interactions per year. TestPilot's approach enables brands to host exponentially more research events at similar cost, with teams shifting from three to five research occasions annually to 30 or 50 research occasions per year, with much faster turnaround[26]. For research questions about product selection, pricing strategies, or packaging changes, this represents a fundamental shift in how quickly teams can validate and iterate.

The value of this compression extends beyond speed to encompassing the ability to conduct exploratory research that would be impractical with human subjects. Teams can use synthetic user simulation to front-load the problem space, fine-tune their research questions, and spend their organic research budget where it matters most[6]. Rather than treating synthetic pre-testing and real-world contextual inquiry as competing alternatives, forward-thinking teams deploy them sequentially: synthetic users provide rapid directional reads on which segments are worth investment before expensive fieldwork begins, while real user research delivers the raw, unfiltered human texture that no generative model can fabricate[21]. As one cognitive scientist working with synthetic users observed, the results from AI-generated responses align with real human findings somewhere between 85% and 100% of the time on major topics and consumer needs[21]. While the research team was careful to note this is not a peer-reviewed clinical trial, 85% alignment provides sufficient signal to stop reflexively dismissing the method and instead ask harder, more specific questions about exactly where synthetic users fit into a research stack[21].

The integration of synthetic user testing into continuous integration and continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipelines represents another mechanism for cycle compression. In traditional software development, automated testing in CI/CD is crucial for preventing unnoticed breaking changes, particularly in team environments[1]. The same principle applies to LLM-based systems: teams can implement automated LLM testing at each code change, enabling regression testing at scale[1]. Frameworks like DeepEval enable this by allowing teams to execute test suites in their CI/CD environment, running multi-turn simulations that evaluate whether conversational AI systems maintain quality across iterations[1]. This capability shifts testing from a phase-gate activity conducted before release to a continuous activity integrated throughout development, enabling teams to detect regressions immediately rather than after deployment.

## Academic Research on LLM-as-User Simulation Reliability and Validity

A critical question underlying the adoption of synthetic user simulation is whether and when LLM-generated responses reliably approximate human behavior. Researchers have employed multiple validation strategies to address this question, each with distinct tradeoffs regarding the strength of evidence they produce. The validate-then-simulate approach, increasingly common in academic research, uses jointly labeled datasets to assess the extent to which LLM-based results align with human results, then draws conclusions about the fitness of the LLM for approximating related human behavior in scenarios where human ground truth is unavailable[11]. In the largest such study, researchers found that LLMs replicated the direction and significance of up to 81% of main effects reported in 156 randomly selected original studies, though LLMs also produced significant results for up to 83% of effects that were not significant with human samples depending on the model[11]. This finding highlights both the promise and the peril: LLMs can capture human response patterns, but they can also generate false positives, particularly for effects that do not occur in real populations.

More sophisticated validation approaches employ individual-level accuracy metrics. In one study using in-depth interview data from 1,000 people to construct LLM agent simulations, researchers examined individual-level accuracy, correlation, and mean absolute error in attempts to replicate individual responses to surveys and controlled experiments[11]. Using a measure of accuracy normalized by the individual's ability to replicate their original responses two weeks later—effectively correcting for human baseline consistency—they found that agent simulations were 85% as accurate as the individuals themselves at replicating responses to the General Social Survey[11]. This finding suggests that while synthetic users introduce some noise relative to the same person responding twice, the degradation is modest enough that LLM simulations capture meaningful individual differences in response patterns.

However, recent research has identified important limitations in how well LLMs can simulate human behavior, particularly when personas contain multiple interacting attributes. When LLMs are simultaneously conditioned on multiple demographic attributes—age, education, employment, location—they face interacting constraints that can enable the model to match some attributes while distorting others[36]. Research comparing persona-conditioned LLMs to unconditioned baselines and random guessers, using World Values Survey U.S. microdata as ground truth, found that persona prompting did not produce consistent aggregate gains across models[36]. Most critically, while some items exhibited limited changes in persona conditions, a smaller subset exhibited larger shifts, and subgroup fidelity did not improve consistently across demographic partitions, sometimes degrading noticeably in low-support strata[36]. These findings argue against treating persona prompting as uniformly beneficial, instead suggesting that persona effects are heterogeneous and depend on both the specific item and the population subgroup being simulated[36].

Data leakage represents another significant reliability challenge identified in recent research. In conversational recommendation systems, data leakage occurs when conversational history includes the target item or when the user simulator explicitly discloses item information during interaction[7][32]. Under such conditions, the recommender system can achieve successful recommendations by directly exploiting the leaked information rather than through genuine understanding of user preferences[7]. When researchers controlled for data leakage by testing systems without the leaked information, model performance deteriorated significantly—for instance, when using certain user simulators, recall@50 decreased by 12.0%, 6.8%, 10.3%, and 17.9% across different baseline models[32]. This finding demonstrates that careful experimental design is essential when evaluating systems against simulated users, as data leakage can produce inflated performance estimates that do not translate to real-world effectiveness[32].

Goal alignment represents another critical dimension of simulation fidelity. State-of-the-art LLM-based user simulators, including Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Qwen-2.5-72B-Instruct, struggle to maintain consistent goal alignment in multi-turn conversations, with documented failure rates ranging from 10–40%[20]. This manifests in simulators that drift from their assigned user goals, provide contradictory information across turns, or abandon role-appropriate behavior[20]. Recent work has introduced inference-time steering as a solution, where simulators are provided with their latest goal state before generating each response, explicitly grounding them in their goal progression[20]. This approach improved goal alignment, increasing average success rate by up to 5.4% in some models[20]. More dramatically, systematic evaluation across diverse domains established that 8-billion-parameter models with explicit goal-tracking mechanisms can achieve competitive or superior performance to 70-billion-parameter models lacking such grounding[20]. These findings highlight that scale alone does not solve the consistency problem; instead, architectural choices about how to maintain state across turns prove crucial.

A recent comprehensive study evaluated state-of-the-art LLMs on the task of generating realistic web actions, using a large-scale real-world dataset of 31,865 user sessions from 3,526 users on an online shopping platform[31]. Each session comprised timestamp-aligned context-action pairs, where context reflected the webpage observed and actions denoted user inputs like clicks, searches, or session termination[31]. When evaluating LLMs without fine-tuning using exact-match accuracy for action generation, most models achieved accuracy below 15%, with reasoning-focused models like DeepSeek-R1 reaching only 11.86%[31]. However, fine-tuning LLMs on domain-specific real-world behavioral data substantially improved performance: Qwen 2.5-7B achieved 17.26% accuracy after fine-tuning, and incorporating synthesized reasoning traces into model training led to additional performance gains[31]. While these absolute accuracy numbers may appear modest, they must be understood in context: the researchers note that predicting user actions precisely is genuinely difficult, and incremental improvements through fine-tuning represent meaningful advances toward more realistic behavior simulation[31].

## Multi-Turn Consistency and Persona Drift in Conversational Contexts

A key dimension of simulation quality that only manifests in multi-turn interactions is persona consistency. Off-the-shelf LLMs often drift from their assigned personas, contradict earlier statements, or abandon role-appropriate behavior across extended conversations[43]. Recent work introduced a unified framework for evaluating and improving persona consistency in LLM-generated dialogue by defining three automatic metrics: prompt-to-line consistency (alignment between the initial persona prompt and the agent's utterances), line-to-line consistency (alignment of statements across consecutive turns), and Q&A consistency (consistency when directly queried about persona attributes)[43]. Using these metrics as reward signals for multi-turn reinforcement learning to fine-tune LLMs for three different user roles (patient, student, and social chat partner), researchers reduced inconsistency by over 55%, resulting in more coherent and faithful simulated users[43]. This demonstrates that persona consistency is not an inherent property of LLMs but rather something that can be systematically improved through targeted training approaches.

The challenge of maintaining behavioral consistency across extended interactions has also led to the development of specialized evaluation frameworks. RPA-Check, a multi-stage automated evaluation framework designed to objectively assess performance of LLM-based role-playing agents in complex, constraints-heavy environments, employs a four-step pipeline: dimension definition (establishing high-level qualitative behavioral criteria), augmentation (expanding requirements into granular boolean checklist indicators), semantic filtering (ensuring indicator objectivity and no redundancy), and LLM-as-a-judge evaluation (employing chain-of-thought verification to score agent fidelity)[44]. Testing this framework on LLM Court, a serious game for forensic training, across five distinct legal scenarios revealed an inverse relationship between parametric scale and procedural consistency—smaller, adequately instruction-tuned 8–9 billion parameter models outperformed larger architectures prone to user-alignment bias or sycophancy[44]. This finding challenges the common assumption that bigger models are always better for simulation tasks, suggesting instead that model size must be matched appropriately to the simulation task and that instruction-tuning quality matters significantly.

## Dynamic Persona Generation Versus Fixed Persona Definition

One of the fundamental design decisions in setting up synthetic user testing involves whether to pre-define detailed personas before testing or to generate personas dynamically based on testing objectives and incoming data. Each approach offers distinct tradeoffs. Fixed personas, carefully constructed from user research, analytics data, and domain expertise, provide consistency and enable teams to test reproducible variations in how different user types interact with products[5]. By defining personas with specific demographics, behaviors, goals, pain points, and technological preferences before testing begins, teams create a stable baseline against which multiple iterations of a product can be evaluated, enabling valid comparison across versions[34]. This approach aligns with traditional persona development practices and integrates naturally into existing design workflows where designers and researchers have already invested effort in creating detailed user archetypes.

However, dynamic persona generation offers complementary advantages. By leveraging AI to analyze behavioral data, interview transcripts, survey responses, and analytics logs, teams can generate personas that reflect the actual distribution of user characteristics rather than cherry-picked archetypes[9][28]. AI-driven clustering tools can group audiences based on demographics, purchase patterns, and behavioral variables, constantly refining segments as fresh data flows in, allowing personas to evolve with user trends[5]. This adaptability helps product teams keep their empathy maps current and ensures that personas remain grounded in up-to-date user behavior rather than becoming stale representations of outdated patterns. For early-stage products or rapidly evolving markets, this continuous refinement capability provides significant value.

A balanced approach combines both strategies: use machine-generated personas for rapid hypothesis generation informed by data, then ground them in observation and ongoing data collection[5]. For early-stage founders and product leaders, using AI for user persona generation represents a powerful addition to the toolkit when applied wisely. Specifically, one recommended methodology involves a staged pipeline where each step produces an inspectable intermediate output[21]. A persona you cannot audit at the extraction, clustering, and validation stages will quietly embed errors that compound all the way into creative briefs and product decisions. The process begins with clarifying objectives and scope—deciding which decisions will rely on personas, what data sources are available, and how many personas are needed[5]. Teams then gather and prepare data from surveys, interviews, analytics logs, CRM records, and market research, combining quantitative data (analytics, purchase patterns) with qualitative insights (interviews), while checking for biases or gaps[5].

Following data preparation, teams select appropriate tools or approaches—either general-purpose large language models like ChatGPT and Claude or specialized research platforms with direct analytics and CRM integration[5]. Teams then prompt and generate initial persona outlines with clear context, asking for specific fields like name, age, occupation, goals, pain points, behavioral traits, and technology preferences[5]. The critical step of adding depth and validation involves layering in behavioral and analytics data to cross-check the persona's pain points against actual user-journey metrics—are users actually abandoning during onboarding, and are there support tickets confirming the pain?[5] Finally, personas should be labeled to distinguish assumptions from facts so that stakeholders know which parts need further research[5]. This approach treats personas as hypotheses to be tested against reality rather than definitive representations, maintaining intellectual honesty about the quality of underlying data.

## Technical Implementation: Persona Conditioning, Multi-Turn Evaluation, and State Tracking

Implementing effective synthetic user simulation requires careful attention to several technical dimensions beyond simply prompt-engineering LLMs to roleplay as users. One critical technical consideration involves how to condition LLM responses on persona information. Different approaches to persona conditioning produce different tradeoffs between fidelity and reliability. Simple zero-shot conditioning—including demographic information in a prompt without fine-tuning—produces some persona effects but often proves unreliable across multiple turns[42]. Experiments testing demographic-based personas on LLM responses to sexism classification tasks found that incorporating gender-based personas in prompts produced inconsistent and unpredictable effects. While some models improved alignment with specific demographic groups in some conditions, others showed decreased agreement, meaning that demographic-based prompting cannot be relied upon as a universally effective way of mitigating bias. These findings argue for more sophisticated approaches to persona conditioning that go beyond simple demographic instructions.

Fine-tuning LLMs on behavioral data specific to target demographics produces more reliable persona effects than zero-shot conditioning. A study building SocSci210, a standardized dataset comprising 2.9 million individual responses from more than 400,000 participants across 210 social science experiments, found that fine-tuning LLMs directly on individual-level responses from past experiments meaningfully improves accuracy of behavioral simulations across diverse social science domains[28]. Their strongest model, Socrates-Qwen-14B, produced predictions 26% more aligned with distributions of human responses to diverse outcome questions under varying conditions relative to its base model, while outperforming GPT-4o by 13%[28]. By fine-tuning on a subset of conditions in a study, generalization to new unseen conditions proved particularly robust, improving by 71%, and fine-tuning reduced demographic parity (a measure of bias) by 10.6%[28]. This demonstrates that behavioral simulation reliability improves substantially through targeted fine-tuning on relevant behavioral data rather than relying on foundation model generalization.

Multi-turn evaluation requires architectural considerations around how to maintain consistency of simulated user goals, preferences, and knowledge across conversation turns. Naive approaches that simply prompt an LLM to generate the next user utterance at each turn often suffer from goal drift: the user simulator loses sight of what it was supposed to accomplish or contradicts earlier statements[20]. Approaches implementing explicit goal state tracking and providing that state to the model before each generation substantially improve goal adherence. When researchers provided LLM-based user simulators with their latest goal state at each turn before generation, goal alignment improved, with average success rate increasing by up to 5.4% in some models[20]. This architectural choice—explicitly maintaining and feeding back goal state—represents a simple but effective engineering practice that substantially improves simulation fidelity without requiring model retraining.

State persistence across turns extends beyond user goals to include conversation history, user-stated preferences, and factual information provided earlier in the conversation. Some simulation frameworks implement sliding-window approaches where evaluation considers a fixed number of prior turns as context, enabling turn-level evaluation metrics like relevancy and consistency[35]. Others track full conversation history, enabling conversation-level metrics like knowledge retention (does the chatbot remember what the user told it earlier?) and role adherence (does the simulated user stay consistent with their assigned persona?)[35]. The choice between these approaches depends on the properties being tested: single-item consistency can be evaluated with sliding windows, while detecting systematic drift across extended conversations requires full history tracking.

The technical distinction between turn-level and conversation-level evaluation frameworks has important implications for simulation validity. Conversation-level evaluation examines the entire interaction holistically, assessing whether the simulated user's behavior across all turns aligns with expected patterns[35]. Turn-level evaluation with sliding context loops through each turn individually, evaluating it using a window of prior context[35]. Most production systems benefit from both: turn-level evaluation catches local inconsistencies and failures to maintain relevance at specific points, while conversation-level evaluation catches systematic drift and goal misalignment that only becomes apparent across extended interaction[35]. This multilayered approach to evaluation enables more comprehensive validation of simulation fidelity than either approach alone.

## Challenges and Limitations: Data Leakage, Bias, and Model Collapse

While synthetic user simulation offers significant advantages, important challenges and limitations constrain its applicability. Data leakage, where simulators or test contexts inadvertently reveal information that enables systems to achieve task goals without genuine understanding, represents a pervasive problem that can produce substantially inflated performance estimates. In conversational recommendation systems, leakage occurs in conversational history (when annotated history includes the target item) and in simulator replies (when user simulators explicitly disclose item information)[7][32]. Under such conditions, recommender systems can achieve successful recommendations by directly exploiting the leaked information rather than through genuine preference understanding. When researchers controlled for data leakage by evaluating on non-leaked examples, model performance dropped substantially[32]. This finding underscores that careful experimental design is essential when evaluating systems against simulated users—data leakage can silently produce inflated performance estimates that evaporate under real-world conditions where such information leakage does not occur.

Bias in synthetic user simulation presents another significant challenge. LLM-generated personas have shown themselves to exhibit demographic biases, with researchers identifying biases in age and occupation representations, and a strong tendency toward personas from the United States. More specifically, LLMs show consistent gender-based biases when asked to classify content through personas aligned with different demographic groups, with higher agreement with female than male annotators on average. Attempting to mitigate these biases through demographic instruction in prompts produces inconsistent and unpredictable effects: some models improve alignment with specific demographic groups in some conditions while others show decreased agreement. These findings argue against assuming that demographic-based persona prompting provides a reliable way of mitigating bias in LLMs. Instead, more fundamental approaches to ensuring representative training data and systematic testing across demographic groups appear necessary.

The synthetic training data quality collapse phenomenon represents perhaps the most profound challenge to the long-term viability of synthetic user simulation at scale[3][3]. Model collapse describes the progressive, self-reinforcing degradation that occurs when LLMs train on data generated by other LLMs[3].
